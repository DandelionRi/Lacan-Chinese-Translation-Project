<span class="smallcaps">引言</span>

<!-- id: s2-17-0006 -->

*我们在此发表的研讨课记录于1955年4月26日发表。这是我们全年专门对「超越快乐原则」所作评注的一个时刻。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0007 -->

*众所周知，弗洛伊德的作品被许多以精神分析学家头衔授权自己的人毫不犹豫地斥为多余的思辨，乃至冒险的臆想，而我们可以从这一例外对立——即死亡本能的概念——来衡量它在多大程度上对大多数人来说是不可思议的——请恕我直言。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0008 -->

*然而，将预示着新拓扑学的作品——即自我、本我和超我这些术语所代表的新拓扑学——视为弗洛伊德学说的出行，更不消说是失足，那却是困难的。这些术语在理论运用中如同在大众传播中一样流行。这简单的领会，在深入那些使所述思辨与构成它的理论修正相联结的动机时，便得到了证实。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0009 -->

*这样一个过程不容置疑地表明了上述术语的误用乃至曲解已经打击了它们的当下使用，这一点已很明显，因为对理论家和对普通人来说，这种使用是完全等价的。毫无疑问，这正证明了那些自我标榜者的说法为正当：即在这些术语中找到了使精神分析经验回归他们所称的普通心理学的媒介。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0010 -->

*在此我们只设定一些标志。重复自动性，Wiederholungszwang……*

<!-- id: s2-17-0011 -->

> *尽管这一概念在相关作品中呈现出来，是为了回应某些临床悖论，诸如创伤性神经症的梦境，其中消极治疗反应*

<!-- id: s2-17-0012 -->

*……不能被视为对教义体系的补充，即使它确实居于其巅。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0013 -->

*这是弗洛伊德在其中重新确认的其开创性发现：即他的「无意识」所蕴含的记忆概念。新事实是他借此以更严格的形式重组它，给予它一种泛化的形式，同时也重新开启其问题域，以对抗那种从那时起就感受到的降解——即将其效果视为简单的既定。* *这里革新的内容，已经在「方案」* [^22] *中有所表述，在那里他的预见勾勒出了他的研究应循的途径：* *Ψ系统，即无意识的前身，在那里显示出其独特性：它只能通过重新找到根本上已失去的对象来满足自己。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0014 -->

*因此，弗洛伊德从一开始就处于克尔凯郭尔所教导的对立中：关于存在的概念，根据它是以回忆还是以重复为根基。如果克尔凯郭尔在其中出色地辨别了古代人与现代人观念的差异，那么弗洛伊德则使后者迈出了决定性的一步：将从代理人性等同于意识，将这一重复中所蕴含的必然性窃去。由于这种重复是象征性的重复，符号的秩序不再能被构想为由人所构成的，而是构成性的。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0015 -->

*因此，我们感到被迫真正地训练听众掌握弗洛伊德作品中所蕴含的回忆概念：鉴于经验教训太过明显，如果让它保持含蓄，分析的数据就会在空中飘浮。* *正是由于弗洛伊德在其经验的原创性上不肯让步，我们看到他被迫在其中唤起一个统治着它的、超出生命之外的元素，他称之为死亡本能。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0016 -->

*弗洛伊德在此给予其后继者——自称后继者——的指示，只能激怒那些在冈纪约式简洁公式所说的——理性沉睡中由其孕育的怪物的人。因为为了不失其惯常姿态，弗洛伊德只向我们提供一个伴随着例子的概念，而这个例子在此将以令人眼花缭乱的方式揭示它所指涉的基本形式化。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0017 -->

*孩子通过让自己的视线中的某个对象消失、再把它带回来、然后再次抹去它来练习的这个游戏——此外这个对象就其本质而言是无关紧要的——与此同时他用不同的音节来调节这种交替——这个游戏，可以说，从其根本特征中显示了人类动物从象征秩序中所接受的决定。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0018 -->

*人literally将自己的时间用于展开Presence和absence获得各自呼应的结构性替代。在其本质结合的时刻，可以说，在慾望的零点时刻，人类对象落入了一种攫取的命运之下，这种攫取取消了其天然属性，从此将其置于符号条件的支配之下。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0019 -->

*事实上，这只是一个洞察，它照亮了个人进入一种秩序的入口，这种秩序以语言的形式支持和接纳他，并在历时性和共时性上将对能指的确定叠加于所指定之上。人们可以从其涌现本身把握到这种超决定——在弗洛伊德对符号功能的感知中，这正是唯一的关切所在。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0020 -->

*那么人们将发现，尽管这个约定恢复了四个符号α、β、γ、δ之间组合机会的严格平等，与先前约定中符号（2）的机会与其他两个符号的机会相等时所累积的歧义相反，* *然而，α、β、γ、δ之间可以称为真正的句法性联结决定了在α和γ之间以及β和δ之间完全不对称的分配可能性。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0036 -->

*事实上，既然已确认任何一个这些术语可以立即跟随任何其他术语，也可以在从其中一个开始计数的第四拍时达到，那么实际上与第三代相反，即每两个符号的结合，服从于一种排斥法则：该法则要求，从α或δ出发，只能得到α或β；从β或γ出发，只能得到γ或δ。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0048 -->

- *和一个遥远的未来，在其彼岸……它们处于明显的非决定中，但只要我的计划决定这个遥远的未来，使我的即时未来变成先将来时，并与我的过去的将来决定相结合，这将从我与我计划实现之间的间隔中排斥* \[虚拟语气\] *符号四分之一的可能性，我这个计划位于其中。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0049 -->

*能指的这种caput mortuum* [^27] *可以被视为任何主体历程的特征。但正是符号决定序列使我们能够正确定位一个主体性的决定，人们通常错误地将其与现实的关系混为一谈。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0050 -->

*为此，在我们的第一个公式中容易推断出的后果里驻足，并非不好，例如在α、β、γ、δ的链条中可以遇到两个连续的β，它们要么直接（ββ），要么在若干αγ对的插入之后（βαγα……γβ），但第二个β之后，在δ出现之前，链条中不会再有新的β出现。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0051 -->

*然而，上述两个β的连续不能再现，除非第二个δ添加到第一个上，其结合方式（在αγ对翻转为γα的逆转附近）等同于强加给两个β的方式。由此立即得出我们在上面宣布的链条不同符号出现概率的不对称。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0052 -->

*而α和γ实际上可以通过一个幸运的偶然序列重复到覆盖整个链条，但即使在最有利的机会下，β和δ也只能以严格等价的术语增加其比例——这将其最大频率限制在50%。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0053 -->

*假设β和δ的组合打击的概率等同于假设α和γ的概率，而实际的打击抽取另一方面严格留给偶然，那么人们看到从现实 中浮现出一种符号决定，这种决定虽然是现实任何偏partiality可以记录的相同之处，但它在其独特的差异中是先存的。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0054 -->

*此外，可以通过简单地考虑两个表Ω和O的结构对比来显示这种差异，即根据联结极端的表是直接还是交叉的排除特征。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0055 -->

*因此人们观察到，如果中间对和极端对可以是相同的，如果最后一个出现在表O中* *（如δδββ，δδαα，ααββ，ββγγ，ββδδ，γγγγ，γγδδ，乃至αααα，是<u>可能的</u>），它们就不能相同如果最后一个出现在*

<!-- id: s2-17-0056 -->

*表Ω的方向（ααγγ，ααδδ，δδγγ，ββββ，ββαα，γγββ，γγαα，<u>不可能的</u>，当然还有δδδδ，参见上文）。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0057 -->

*符号决定的另一个例子，其娱乐性特征不应使我们迷失方向。因为除了这种符号决定之外，没有其他联结可以定位弗洛伊德为我们带来的符号性超决定概念，而且在一个像他这样的精神中，它从来不能被构想为现实性超决定——他所有的矛盾都使他屈从于这种概念 aberration，那些哲学家和医生们太容易在其中找到平息其宗教热情的借口。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0058 -->

*符号自主性的这种立场是使精神分析中自由联想的理论和实践摆脱其歧义的唯一方法。因为将弹簧归之于符号决定及其法则，与归之于支持联想主义——哲学的或伪称实验的——的惰性想象预设，是完全不同的事情。由于放弃了审查，精神分析学家们在这里找到了一个更多的呼唤点，以支持他们不断陷入的心理化混淆，那些有意为之的人。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0059 -->

*事实上，只有这些例子——在链条的符号要求被无限期悬置的延续中——才使我们能够构思无意识慾望在其坚不可摧的持久性中的位置——尽管在弗洛伊德学说中它显得矛盾，但这却是其中最被强调的特征之一。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0060 -->

I was in Paris in 18... After a dark and stormy autumn evening, I was enjoying the double voluptuousness of meditation and a pipe of meerschaum, in company with my friend Dupin, in his little library or study, rue Dunot, No. 33, au troisième, faubourg Saint-Germain. Did you dismantle all the chairs? – Certainly not; but we did better, we examined the rungs of every chair in the hotel, and indeed the joints of every description of furniture, by the aid of a most powerful microscope. Any disorder in the glueing – a simple gaping in the joints – would have sufficed to reveal the hiding place. – I presume you have examined the mirrors, between the board and the plate, and you have probed the beds and bed clothes, as well as the curtains and carpets. – Naturally; and when we had absolutely completed every particle of furniture in this way, we examined the house itself. We divided the whole of its surface into compartments, which we numbered, so that none might be missed; we made each square inch of the premises an object of renewed examination under the microscope, and we included the two houses immediately adjoining. – The two houses adjoining! I exclaimed; you must have given yourselves infinite trouble. – We did; but the reward offered is enormous. – In the houses, you understand, the floor? – The floor is everywhere paved with brick. This did not give us very much trouble comparatively. We examined the moss between the bricks, and found it undisturbed. – You have no doubt visited D...'s papers, and the books of the library. – Certainly; we opened every parcel and every package; we not only opened every book, but turned over every leaf in each volume, not contenting ourselves with simply shaking them, as some of our police officers do. We also measured the thickness of every book cover with the most exact minuteness, and applied to each the most jealous scrutiny of the microscope. Had any of the bindings been recently meddled with, it would have been absolutely impossible for the fact to have escaped our observation. Some five or six volumes, just from the hands of the binder, we carefully probed longitudinally with the needles. – You explored the floors beneath the carpets? – Beyond doubt. We removed every carpet, and examined the boards with the microscope. – And the paper on the walls? – Yes. – You looked into the cellars? – We did look into the cellars. – Then, said I, you have been making a miscalculation, and the letter is not in the hotel, as you suppose. – I fear you are right, said the Prefect. – And you now, Dupin, what do you advise me to do? – To make a thorough re-search. – That is absolutely unnecessary! replied G... As sure as I live, the letter is not in the hotel! – I have no better advice to give you, said Dupin. You have, of course, an accurate description of the letter. – Oh! yes! And here, the Prefect, producing a memorandum book, began to read aloud a minute description of the lost document, of its internal, and especially of its external appearance. Soon after finishing the reading of this description, this excellent man took his departure, more completely depressed in spirits than I had ever known him before. About a month afterward, he paid us another visit, and found us occupied very nearly as before. He took a pipe and a chair, and entered into some ordinary conversation. At length, I said to him: – Well, but G..., and your purloined letter? I presume you have at last made up your mind that it is no easy matter to get the better of the minister? – The devil take him! – I have, it is true, renewed the search, as Dupin advised me; but it was all labor lost, as I suspected it would be. – How much was the reward offered, did you say? asked Dupin. – Why, it is very large – a truly magnificent reward – I do not like to say exactly how much; but one thing I will say, that I would not mind giving my own check for fifty thousand francs to anyone who could find this letter for me. The fact is, the thing becomes daily more urgent, and the reward has been lately doubled. But, in truth, were it trebled, I could do no more than I have done. – But... yes... said Dupin, drawling out his words amid the puffs of his pipe, I really believe, G..., that you have not exerted yourself... to the utmost in this matter. You might do a little more, I think, eh? – How? in what sense? – Why... (a puff of smoke) you might... (puff upon puff) – take counsel in the matter, eh? – (Three puffs of smoke.) – Do you remember the story they tell of Abernethy? – No! hang Abernethy! – To be sure! hang him and welcome. But, once upon a time, a certain rich, very miserly man conceived the design of spunging upon this Abernethy for a medical opinion. For this purpose, he introduced a common conversation in private company, through which he insinuated his case to the physician, as that of an imaginary individual. – We will suppose, said the miser, that his symptoms are such and such; now, Doctor, what would you have him take? – Take! said Abernethy, why, take advice, to be sure. – But, said the Prefect, a little taken aback, I am perfectly willing to take advice, and to pay for it. I would really give fifty thousand francs to anyone who would aid me in the matter. – In that case, replied Dupin, opening a drawer and producing a check book, you may as well fill me up a check for the amount mentioned. When you have signed it, I will hand you your letter. I was astounded. As for the Prefect, he seemed absolutely thunderstricken. For some minutes he remained speechless and motionless, looking incredulously at my friend with open mouth and eyes that seemed starting from their sockets; then, apparently recovering himself somewhat, he seized a pen, and after several pauses and vacant stares, finally filled up and signed a check for fifty thousand francs, and handed it across the table to Dupin. The latter examined it carefully and deposited it in his pocketbook; then, unlocking an escritoire, took therefrom a letter and gave it to the Prefect. Our functionary grasped it in perfect agony of joy, opened it with a trembling hand, cast a rapid glance at its contents, and then, scrambling and struggling to the door, rushed unceremoniously from the room and from the house, without having uttered a syllable since Dupin had requested him to fill up the check. When he had gone, my friend entered into some explanations. – The Parisian police, said he, are excessively able in their way. Their agents are persevering, ingenious, cunning, and thoroughly versed in the knowledge which their duties seem chiefly to demand. Thus, when G... detailed to us his mode of searching the premises at the D... hotel, I felt entire confidence in his having made a satisfactory investigation, so far as his labors extended. – So far as his labors extended? said I. – Yes, said Dupin; the measures adopted were not only the best of their kind, but carried out to absolute perfection. Had the letter been deposited within the range of their search, these fellows would, beyond question, have found it. I merely laughed; but Dupin seemed to have said this quite seriously. – The measures, then, continued he, were good in their kind and well executed; their defect lay in their being inapplicable to the case and to the man. A certain set of highly ingenious resources are, with the Prefect, a sort of Procrustean bed, to which he forcibly adapts his designs. But he perpetually errs by being too deep or too shallow for the matter in hand; and many a schoolboy would reason better than he. I knew a boy about eight years old, whose success at guessing in the game of "even and odd" attracted universal admiration. This game is simple; it is played with marbles. One player holds in his hand a certain number of these toys, and asks the other whether that number is even or odd. If the guess is right, the guesser wins one; if wrong, he loses one. The boy of whom I speak won all the marbles of the school. Naturally, he had a principle of guessing; and this lay in the mere observation and appreciation of the astuteness of his opponents. For example, suppose his opponent is a perfect simpleton: the boy, holding up his closed hand, asks: "Are they even or odd?" Our schoolboy replies: "Odd!" and loses; but upon the second trial he wins, for he then says to himself: "The simpleton had them even upon the first trial, and his amount of cunning is just sufficient to make him have them odd upon the second; I will therefore guess odd;" he guesses "Odd," and wins. Now, with a simpleton a degree above the first, he would have reasoned thus: "This fellow finds that in the first instance I guessed odd, and in the second he will propose to himself, upon the first impulse, a simple variation from even to odd, as did the first simpleton; but then a second thought will suggest that this is too simple a variation, and finally he will decide upon putting them even as before. I will therefore guess even;" he guesses "Even," and wins. Now this mode of reasoning in the schoolboy, whom his fellows called "lucky" – what, in its last analysis, is it? – It is merely, said I, an identification of the reasoner's intellect with that of his opponent. – It is, said Dupin; and, upon inquiring of this little boy by what means he effected this thorough identification which constituted his whole success, I received answer as follows: "When I wish to find out how wise, or how stupid, or how good, or how wicked any one is, or what are his thoughts at the moment, I fashion the expression of my face as accurately as possible in accordance with the expression of his, and then wait to see what thoughts or sentiments arise in my mind or heart, as if to match or correspond with my expression." This response of the schoolboy lies at the bottom of all the spurious profundity which has been attributed to La Rochefoucauld, to La Bruyère, to Machiavelli, and to Campanella. – And the identification of the reasoner's intellect with that of his opponent depends, if I understand you aright, upon the accuracy with which the opponent's intellect is admeasured. – For its practical value, it does depend upon this, replied Dupin; and, if the Prefect and his whole band have so often failed, it is, first, for want of this identification, and secondly, by an inaccurate admeasurement, or rather by a non-admeasurement, of the intellect with which they are dealing. They consider only their own ideas of ingenuity; and, when seeking something hidden, they advert only to the modes in which they themselves would have hidden it. They are right in this, that their own ingenuity is a faithful representative of that of the mass; but when there is a particular felon whose cunning differs in kind from their own, that felon, of course, defeats them. This always happens when his cunning is above their own, and very usually when it is below. They have no variation of principle in their investigations; at most, when urged by some unusual case – by some extraordinary reward – they extend or exaggerate their old practices, but they change nothing in their principles. In the case of D..., for example, what has been done to change the principle of operation? What is all this boring, and probing, and sounding, and scrutinizing with the microscope, and dividing the surface of the building into numbered square inches? What is all this, if not an exaggeration, in its application, of one principle or set of principles of search, which are based upon one set of notions regarding human ingenuity, to which the Prefect, in the long routine of his duty, has been accustomed? Do you not see that he has taken it for granted that all men who wish to conceal a letter – not exactly in a gimlet hole bored in a chair leg – but at least in some out-of-the-way hole or corner suggested by the same tenor of thought which would urge a man to secrete a letter in a gimlet hole bored in a chair leg? And do you not see also, that such recherche nooks for concealment are adapted only for ordinary occasions, and would be adopted only by ordinary intellects? For, in all cases of concealment, this ambitious and tortured manner of disposing the article – this manner of disposing it in this recherche manner – is, in the very first instance, presumable and presumed; and thus its discovery depends, not at all upon acumen, but altogether upon the mere care, patience, and determination of the seekers; and where the case is of importance, or what amounts to the same thing in the eyes of the police, when the reward is of magnitude, the qualities in question have never been known to fail. You will now understand what I meant in suggesting that, had the purloined letter been hidden anywhere within the limits of the Prefect's examination – in other words, had the principle of its concealment been comprehended within the principles of the Prefect – its discovery would have been a matter altogether beyond question. This functionary, however, has been thoroughly mystified; and the remote source of his defeat lies in the supposition that the Minister is a fool, because he has acquired a reputation as a poet. All fools are poets – this the Prefect feels – and he is merely guilty of a non distributio medii in thence inferring that all poets are fools. – But is this really the poet? I asked. I know there are two brothers, and both have made a reputation in literature. The minister, I believe, has written learnedly on the differential calculus. He is the mathematician, and not the poet. – You are mistaken; I know him well; he is both poet and mathematician. As poet and mathematician, he would reason correctly; as mere mathematician, he could not have reasoned at all, and thus would have been at the mercy of the Prefect. – Such an opinion, said I, is fitted to astonish me; it is contradicted by the voice of the whole world. You do not mean to set at naught the well-digested idea of centuries. Mathematical reason has long been regarded as the reason par excellence. – There is a bet, replied Dupin, quoting Chamfort, that every political idea, every received convention is a piece of folly, for it has suited the greatest number. The mathematicians – I grant you that – have done their best to propagate the popular error of which you speak, and which, although propagated as truth, is nonetheless a perfect error. For example, they have, with a art worthy a better cause, accustomed us to apply the term "analysis" to algebraic operations. The French are the prime culprits in this scientific fraud; but if the terms of language are of real importance – if words derive their value from their applicability – oh! then, I concede that analysis translates algebra, about as much as, in Latin, "ambitus" implies ambition, "religio" religion, or "homines honesti" the class of honorable men.

<!-- id: s2-17-0279 -->

I see, said I, that you are going to pick a quarrel with a good many of the algebraists of Paris; but continue. – I dispute the validity, and consequently the results, of that reason which is cultivated by any special process other than abstract logic. I dispute in particular the reason educed by mathematical study. Mathematics is the science of form and quantity; mathematical reasoning is merely logic applied to observation upon form and quantity. The great error lies in supposing that even the truths of what is called pure algebra are abstract or general truths. And this error is so egregious that I am confounded at the universality with which it has been received. Mathematical axioms are not axioms of general truth. What is true of relation – of form and quantity – is often grossly false in regard to morals, for example. In this latter science, it is very usually untrue that the aggregated parts are equal to the whole. In chemistry also the axiom fails. In the consideration of motive power, it likewise fails; for two motives, each of a given value, have not, necessarily, when united, a value equal to the sum of their values apart. There are numerous other mathematical truths which are only truths within the limits of relation. But the mathematician argues from his finite truths, from habit, as if they were of absolutely general applicability – as the world indeed imagines them to be. Bryant, in his very learned Mythology, mentions an analogous source of errors, when he says that, although no one believes the pagan fables, yet we forget ourselves continually, and make inferences from them as if they were living realities. With the algebraists themselves, who are themselves pagans, the pagan fables are believed, and the inferences are made, not so much through lapse of memory, as through an unaccountable addling of the brains. In short, I never yet encountered the mere mathematician in whom one could have confidence out of equal roots, nor one who did not clandestinely hold it as an article of faith that x² + px is absolutely and unconditionally equal to q. Say to one of these gentlemen, by way of experiment, if you please, that you believe occasions may occur where x² + px would not be altogether equal to q; and, having made him understand what you mean, get beyond his reach as speedily as convenient; for, beyond doubt, he will endeavor to knock you down.

I mean to say, continued Dupin, while I merely laughed at his last observations, that, if the minister had been nothing more than a mathematician, the Prefect would not have been under the necessity of making me this check. I knew him to be both mathematician and poet, and I took my measures in reason of his capacity, and in accordance with the circumstances in which he found himself placed. I knew him to be a courtier and a bold intrigant. Such a man, I considered, could not fail to be aware of the ordinary modes of police action. He could not have failed to anticipate – and events have proved that he did not fail to anticipate – the ambushes which were prepared for him. I reflected that he had foreseen the secret investigations of his premises. His frequent absences from home at night, which our good Prefect had saluted as positive aids to his future success, I regarded merely as ruses to afford opportunity for thorough search to the police, and thus the sooner to impress them with the conviction to which G... in fact did finally arrive – the conviction that the letter was not upon the premises. I felt, also, that the whole train of thought, which I was at some pains in detailing to you just now, concerning the invariable principles of police action in searches for concealed articles... ideas which I explained to you not without some trouble... I felt, I say, that this whole train of thought would necessarily have passed through the mind of the minister. This would imperatively have led him to despise all the ordinary nooks of concealment. He could not, I reflected, have been so weak as not to see that the most intricate and remote recess of his hotel would be as open, as the most common closets, to the eyes, to the probes, to the gimlets, and to the microscopes of the Prefect. I saw, in fine, that he would have been driven, as a matter of course, to simplicity, if he had not been induced to it as a matter of choice. You will remember perhaps with what bursts of laughter the Prefect received the idea which I expressed in our first interview, that, if the mystery troubled him so much, it was perhaps on account of its being so very self-evident. – Yes, said I, I remember his merriment well. I really thought he would have fallen into convulsions. – The material world, continued Dupin, abounds with very strict analogies to the immaterial; and thus some color of truth has been given to the rhetorical dogma, that metaphor, or simile, may be made to strengthen an argument, as well as to embellish a description. The principle of the vis inertiae, for example, seems to be identical in both physics and metaphysics. It is not more true in the former that a large body is with more difficulty set in motion than a smaller one, and that its subsequent momentum is commensurate with this difficulty, than it is, in the latter, that intellects of vaster capacity, while more forcible, more constant, and more eventful in their movements than those of inferior grade, are yet the less readily moved, and more embarrassed and full of hesitation in the first few steps of their progress. Again: have you ever noticed which of the street signs, over the shop doors, are the most attractive of attention? – I have never given the matter a thought, said I. – There is a game of puzzles, he resumed, which is played upon a map. One party playing requires another to find a given word – the name of a town, river, state or empire – in short, any word whatsoever, upon the motley and perplexed surface of the chart. A novice in the game generally seeks to embarrass his opponents by giving them the most minutely lettered names; but the adept selects such words as stretch, in large characters, from one end of the chart to the other. These, like the over-largely lettered signs and placards of the street, escape observation by dint of being excessively obvious; and here the physical oversight is precisely analogous with the moral inapprehension by which the intellect suffers to pass unnoticed those considerations which are too obtrusively and too palpably self-evident. But this is a point, it appears, somewhat above or beneath the understanding of the Prefect. He never once thought it probable, or possible, that the minister had deposited his letter immediately beneath the nose of the whole world, by way of best preventing any portion of that world from perceiving it.

But the more I reflected upon the daring, dashing, and discriminating ingenuity of D...; upon the fact that the document must always have been at hand, if he intended to use it to good purpose; and upon the decisive evidence, obtained by the Prefect, that it was not hidden within the limits of that dignitary's ordinary search – the more satisfied I became that, to conceal this letter, the minister had resorted to the most comprehensive and sagacious expedient in the world – the expedient of not attempting to conceal it at all.

Full of these ideas, I prepared myself with a pair of green spectacles, and called one fine morning, quite by accident, at the ministerial hotel. I found D... at home, yawning, lounging, and dawdling, as usual, and pretending to be in the last extremity of ennui. D... is perhaps the most really energetic human being now alive, but that is only when nobody sees him. To be even with him, I complained of my weak eyes, and lamented the necessity of the spectacles, under cover of which I cautiously and thoroughly surveyed the whole apartment, while seemingly intent only upon the conversation of my host. I paid especial attention to a large writing-table near which he sat, and upon which lay confusedly some miscellaneous letters and other papers, with one or two musical instruments and a few books. Here, however, after a long and very deliberate scrutiny, I saw nothing to excite particular suspicion. At length my eyes, in going the circuit of the room, fell upon a trumpery fillagree card-rack, that hung dangling by a dirty blue ribbon, from a little brass knob just beneath the middle of the mantel-piece. In this rack, which had three or four compartments, were five or six visiting cards and a solitary letter. This last was much soiled and crumpled. It was torn nearly in two, across the middle – as if a design, in the first instance, to tear it entirely up as worthless, had been altered, or stayed, in the second. It had a large black seal, bearing the D... cipher very conspicuously, and was addressed, in a diminutive female hand, to D..., the minister, himself. It was thrust carelessly, and even, as it seemed, contemptuously, into one of the uppermost divisions of the rack.

No sooner had I glanced at this letter, than I concluded it to be that of which I was in search. To be sure, it was, to all appearance, radically different from the one of which the Prefect had read us so minute a description. Here the seal was large and black, with the D... cipher; there it was small and red, with the ducal arms of the S... family. Here, the address, to the minister, diminutive and feminine; there the superscription, to a certain royal personage, was markedly bold and decided; the size alone formed a point of correspondence. But then the radicalness of these differences, which was excessive; the dirt; the soiled and torn condition of the paper, so inconsistent with the true methodical habits of D..., and so suggestive of a design to delude the beholder into an idea of the worthlessness of the document; these things, together with the hyper-obtrusive situation of this document, full in the view of every visitor, and thus exactly in accordance with the conclusions to which I had previously arrived; these things, I say, were strongly corroborative of suspicion, in one who came with the intention to suspect. I protracted my visit as long as possible, and, while I maintained a most animated discussion with the minister upon a topic which I knew well had never failed to interest and excite him, I kept my attention really riveted upon the letter. In this examination, I committed to memory its external appearance and arrangement in the rack; and also fell, at length, upon a discovery which set at rest whatever trivial doubt I might have entertained. In scrutinizing the edges of the paper, I observed them to be more chafed than seemed necessary. They presented the broken appearance which is manifested when a stiff paper, having been once folded and pressed with a folder, is refolded in a reversed direction, in the same creases or edges which had formed the original fold. This discovery was sufficient. It was clear to me that the letter had been turned, as a glove, inside out, re-directed, and re-sealed. I bade the minister good morning, and took my departure at once, leaving a gold snuff box upon the table.

The next morning I called for the snuff box, and we resumed, quite eagerly, the conversation of the preceding day. But while thus engaged, however, a loud report, as if of a pistol, was heard immediately beneath the windows of the hotel, and was succeeded by a series of fearful screams and the shoutings of a terrified mob. D... rushed to a casement, threw it open, and looked out. In the meantime, I stepped to the card-rack, took the letter, put it in my pocket, and replaced it by a fac-simile, (so far as regards externals,) which I had carefully prepared at my lodgings – imitating the D... cipher, very readily, by means of a seal formed of bread. The disturbance in the street had been occasioned by the frantic behavior of a man with a musket. He had fired it among a crowd of women and children. It proved, however, to have been without ball, and the fellow was suffered to go his way as a lunatic or a drunkard. When he had gone, D... came from the window, whither I had followed him immediately upon securing the object in view. Soon afterward I bade him farewell. The pretended lunatic was a man in my own pay.

But what purpose had you, I asked my friend, in replacing the letter by a fac-simile? Would it not have been better, at the first visit, to have seized it openly and departed? – D..., replied Dupin, is a desperate man, and a man of nerve. His hotel, too, is not without attendants devoted to his interests. Had I made the wild attempt you suggest, I might never have left the ministerial presence alive. The good people of Paris might have heard of me no more. But I had an object apart from these considerations. You know my political prepossessions. In this matter, I act as a partisan of the lady concerned. For eighteen months the minister has had her in his power. She has now him in hers – since, being unaware that the letter is not in his possession, he will proceed with his exactions as if it was. Thus will he inevitably commit himself, at once, to his political destruction. His downfall, too, will not be more precipitate than awkward. It is all very well to talk about the facilis descensus Averni; but in all kinds of climbing, as Catalani said of singing, it is far more easy to get up than to come down. In the present instance I have no sympathy – at least no pity – for him who descends. He is that monstrum horrendum, an unprincipled man of genius. I confess, however, that I should like very well to know the precise character of his thoughts, when, being defied by her whom the Prefect terms "a certain personage," he is reduced to opening the letter which I left for him in the card-rack. – How! did you put anything particular in it? – Why – it did not seem altogether right to leave the interior blank – that would have been insulting. D..., at Vienna once, did me an evil turn, which I told him, quite good-humoredly, that I should remember. So, as I knew he would feel some curiosity in regard to the identity of the person who had outwitted him, I thought it a pity not to give him a clue. At Paris, just after dark one gusty evening in the autumn of 18.., I was enjoying the twofold luxury of meditation and a meerschaum, in company with my friend C. Auguste Dupin, in his little back library, or book-closet, au troisième, No. 33, Rue Dunôt, Faubourg St. Germain. For one hour at least we had maintained a profound silence; while each, to any casual observer, might have seemed intently and exclusively occupied with the curling eddies of smoke that oppressed the atmosphere of the chamber. For myself, however, I was mentally discussing certain topics which had formed matter for conversation between us at an earlier period of the evening; I mean the affair of the Rue Morgue, and the mystery attending the murder of Marie Roget. I looked upon it, therefore, as something of a coincidence, when the door of our apartment was thrown open and admitted our old acquaintance, Monsieur G., the Prefect of the Parisian police. We gave him a hearty welcome; for there was nearly half as much of the entertaining as of the contemptible about the man, and we had not seen him for several years. We had been sitting in the dark, and Dupin now arose for the purpose of lighting a lamp, but sat down again, without doing so, upon G.'s saying that he had called to consult us, or rather to ask the opinion of my friend, about some official business which had occasioned a great deal of trouble. "If it is any point requiring reflection," observed Dupin, as he forebore to enkindle the wick, "we shall examine it to better purpose in the dark." "That is another of your odd notions," said the Prefect, who had a fashion of calling every thing "odd" that was beyond his comprehension, and thus lived amid an absolute legion of "oddities." "Very true," said Dupin, as he supplied his visitor with a pipe, and rolled towards him a comfortable chair. "And what is the difficulty now?" I asked. "Nothing more in the assassination way, I hope?" "Oh no; nothing of that nature. The fact is, the business is *very* simple indeed, and I make no doubt that we can manage it sufficiently well ourselves; but then I thought Dupin would like to hear the details of it, because it is so excessively *odd*." "[Si<span id="Simple_odd" class="anchor"></span>mple and odd](#R_Simple_odd)," said Dupin. "Why, yes; and not exactly that, either. The fact is, we have all been a good deal puzzled because the affair *is* so simple, and yet baffles us altogether." "Perhaps it is the very simplicity of the thing which puts you at fault," said my friend. "What nonsense you *do* talk!" replied the Prefect, laughing heartily. "Perhaps the mystery is a little *too* plain," said Dupin. "Oh, good heavens! who ever heard of such an idea?" "A little *too* self evident." "Ha! ha! ha!-ha! ha! ha!-ho! ho! ho!" roared our visitor, profoundly amused, "oh, Dupin, you will be the death of me yet!" "And what, after all, *is* the matter on hand?" I asked. "Why, I will tell you," replied the Prefect, as he gave a long, steady and contemplative puff, and settled himself in his chair. "I will tell you in a few words; but, before I begin, let me caution you that this is an affair demanding the greatest secrecy, and that I should most probably lose the position I now hold, were it known that I confided it to any one." "Proceed," said I. "Or not," said Dupin. "Well, then; I have received personal information, from a very high quarter, that a certain document of the last importance, has been purloined from the royal apartments. The individual who purloined it is known; this beyond a doubt; he was seen to take it. It is known, also, that it still remains in his possession." "How is this known?" asked Dupin. "It is clearly inferred," replied the Prefect, "from the nature of the document, and from the non-appearance of certain results which would at once arise from its passing out of the robber's possession;-that is to say, from his employing it as he must design in the end to employ it." "Be a little more explicit," I said. "Well, I may venture so far as to say that the paper gives its holder a certain power in a certain quarter where such power is immensely valuable." - 或者对信的作者采取某种行动，他在游戏中保持置身事外这一事实表明，这里所涉及的几乎不是罪行和过错，而是*信所构成的矛盾和丑闻的标记*，按福音书中所说的，它必须发生，而不顾成为其载体的人的灾祸，

<!-- id: s2-17-0218 -->

- 甚至将已经成为*案卷中一份文件*的信交给"第三者"，他有权决定是否就此为王后召开火刑法庭，还是让大臣失势。

<!-- id: s2-17-0221 -->

- 延长其迂回以使它通过一次额外的转运到达应有的人那里，也就是说，通过另一种背叛，由于信的严重性，要防止其回返是困难的，

<!-- id: s2-17-0222 -->

- 或者销毁信，这是唯一可靠的方式，杜潘【DUPIN】一开始就这样说。

<!-- id: s2-17-0224 -->

大臣从局势中获取的威势，因此并不来自信本身，而是——无论他是否知道——来自信为他构成的人物。警长的言论也把他呈现为一个敢于做一切的人：*"*[*who da*<span id="R_who_dares_all_things" class="anchor"></span>*res all things*](#who_dares_all_things)，人们意味深长地评论道：*...those unbecoming as well as those becoming a man"*。这是说：*"不配作为一个人的，正如配作为一个人的"*，而波德莱尔在翻译时漏掉了其精妙之处：*"不配一个人的，正如配得上他的"*。因为在原文形式中，这个评语更适用于"*让一个女人感兴趣的东西*"。这让人看到这个人物的*想象界的范畴*，也就是说大臣所卷入的*自恋关系*，这一次当然是他不知情的。它也由英文文本中第二页叙述者的一句妙趣横生的话所指出：

<!-- id: s2-17-0225 -->

"*他告诉我们，大臣所获取的威势，依赖于劫持者对受害者认识劫持者这件事的认识。*"*原文为：*"*[*the ro*<span id="R_the_robber" class="anchor"></span>*bber's knowledge of the loser's knowledge of the robber.*](#the_robber)*"*

<!-- id: s2-17-0226 -->

作者让杜潘【DUPIN】在紧接着信被劫的场景叙事之后，以原文方式重复这些词，从而强调其重要性。这里我们仍可以说波德莱尔在其语言中游移，他让一个发问，另一个以这些词确认：

<!-- id: s2-17-0227 -->

- *"窃贼知道……"*然后

<!-- id: s2-17-0228 -->

- *"窃贼知道*——什么？——*被偷的人认识他的窃贼。"*

<!-- id: s2-17-0229 -->

因为对窃贼来说重要的不仅仅是该人知道*谁偷了他*，而是就窃贼而言他在与谁打交道，是他相信他能"*做一切*"，这必须理解为：她赋予他一个位置，这个位置没有任何人真正能够承担，因为它是*想象界的*，即绝对主人的位置。

<!-- id: s2-17-0230 -->

实际上这是一个绝对软弱的位置，但并非对于被让人去相信它的人而言。证明这一点不仅仅是王后有勇气求助于警察。因为她所做的只是顺从于她从起始三位一体的一行中位移一格，将自己托付给占据这个位置所必需的盲目本身：

<!-- id: s2-17-0231 -->

"[*No more saga*<span id="R_No_more_sagacious" class="anchor"></span>*cious agent could, I suppose*](#No_more_sagacious)——杜潘讽刺地说——*be desired or even imagined.*"

<!-- id: s2-17-0232 -->

不，她之所以跨出这一步，与其说是如人们所说的被*推向绝望*【*driven to despair*】，不如说是承担了一种不耐烦，这种不耐烦更应该归因于一种镜像的幻象。因为大臣在克制自己不去采取当时他所命定的不行动方面，是有相当多的事要做的。

<!-- id: s2-17-0233 -->

*大臣*实际上*并不完全疯*。这是警长的一句评论，而他总是*说话如金*：诚然，*他话语之金*只为杜潘而流，并且其流动只在*五万法郎*——按当时金属的标准，他为此付出的代价——的额度上才停止，*尽管这不会不给他留下有盈余的余额*。*因此大臣在这种疯狂的停滞中并不完全疯*，*这就是为什么他必须以神经症的方式行动*：

<!-- id: s2-17-0234 -->

- 如同那个退居小岛以遗忘的人——遗忘什么？——他遗忘了……

<!-- id: s2-17-0235 -->

- 如同大臣，因不使用信，反而将它遗忘。

<!-- id: s2-17-0236 -->

他行为的坚持性所表达的正是这一点。但信，和神经症的无意识一样，并不遗忘他。它如此不遗忘他，*以至于它将他*越来越转化为那个以他的惊讶呈上它的人的影像，并且他将以她为榜样，屈从于一种类似的惊讶。

<!-- id: s2-17-0237 -->

这种转化的特征被记录下来，并且以一种在它们表面的无理由中足够特征性的形式，足以有效地与*被压抑者的回归*相比拟。因此我们首先得知*他继而也翻过信*，当然不是以王后匆忙的手势，而是以一种更为精心操作的方式，如同人们翻转一件衣服那样。事实上，根据当时人们*折叠*一封信并*封蜡*的方式，他必须如此操作，以露出空白的部分来写新地址[^46]。这个地址成为他自己的地址。无论是他亲手所写还是由他人代笔，它都将以非常纤细的女性字迹出现，火漆印从激情的红色变到他镜子的黑色，他盖上自己的印章。

<!-- id: s2-17-0238 -->

这种*盖有收信人印章的信*的独特性，在其虚构中越是值得注意，因为它在文本中被强烈地表达出来，而在杜潘关于信的辨识的讨论中却甚至没有被他提及。无论这一遗漏是故意还是无意的，在一种我们看到其精细严密的创作的安排中，它都会令人惊讶。但无论如何，意味深长的是，大臣大致上是写给自己的信，是一封女性的信：仿佛他必须经历一个能指的天然相宜性的阶段。同样地……

<!-- id: s2-17-0239 -->

- *懒散的气场*甚至影响到柔弱的表象，

<!-- id: s2-17-0240 -->

- *无聊的铺陈*在他的言语中近于厌恶，

<!-- id: s2-17-0241 -->

- *氛围*——《*家具哲学*》【La philosophie de l'ameublement】[^47]的作者知道如何通过几乎不可触摸的笔触唤起的，诸如桌上那件乐器的笔触——*……*所有一切都似乎是协调一致的，以至于这个被其所有言语刻画了男性气概的人物，在他出现时散发出最独特的*女性气息*。如果这是一种伎俩，杜潘确实不忘记强调它，他告诉我们，在这种*假象*背后，是猛兽的警觉，准备一跃而起。

<!-- id: s2-17-0242 -->

但这就是无意识本身的效果，在我们教导其精确意义上无意识即是——人被能指所寓居——我们如何能找到比爱伦·坡【POE】自己所锻造来让我们理解杜潘的绝技的更美的形象呢？因为他为此借助了那些*地名*，一张地理地图为了让其不沉默，将其叠加在其图案之上，人们可以将其作为猜谜游戏的对象，看谁能找到一位伙伴所选择的那个；继而注意到最有可能使初学者迷失的，是地图上以宽间距的大写字母印出的、常常甚至目光都不停留的、标示出整个国家名称的那个。

<!-- id: s2-17-0243 -->

如此，*被偷的信*，如同一具庞大的女性身体，在大臣的办公室空间中铺开，当杜潘进入时。但他已预期在此找到它，他只需用*绿色眼镜*所遮掩的眼睛，剥去这具巨大的身体。正因如此，不需要，也因为没有机会，偷听弗洛伊德【FREUD】教授的门口，他会直奔那躺着、且正是这身体被做来藏匿的地方，在某个目光溜进去的美好中点，乃至在被诱惑者称为"*圣天使堡*"的地点，在他们天真地自以为由此把握全城的错觉之中。

<!-- id: s2-17-0244 -->

看！*在壁炉的两柱之间*，是这一伸手可及的对象，劫持者只需伸手去取……问题在于*他是抓住*——正如波德莱尔所译——*在壁炉台上*，还是【在壁炉台*下*】*sous le manteau*，如同原文所载，可以毫无损失地留给厨房的推断。

<!-- id: s2-17-0245 -->

如果*象征的效力*就此停止，是否因为*象征的债务*也已在其中消亡？如果我们能相信这一点，我们将被两个事件所警告其相反——这两个事件我们越不能视为附属，因为它们初看似乎与作品格格不入。首先是杜潘的酬金故事，它远非结尾的玩笑，它在开篇即由他向警长就*许诺给他的酬金数额*所提出的相当放肆的问题所预告，而警长虽然对自己的具体数字有所保留，却不打算向他隐瞒其巨大数额，并在后面回到其增长。

<!-- id: s2-17-0246 -->

杜潘此前被呈现为困居于以太之中的穷人，这一事实反而促使我们反思他在信的交付上所达成的交易，而他的*支票簿*的出现利落地保证了执行。我们并不认为无关紧要的是，他所引入的那个毫不隐晦的*暗示*，是"*归诸一位同样著名而古怪的人物*"——波德莱尔告诉我们——一位名叫阿伯内西【ABERNETHY】的英国医生的故事，其中涉及一个富有的守财奴，他想要从他那里免费获得一次诊治，结果得到的不是让他服药，而是让他接受建议。

<!-- id: s2-17-0247 -->

事实上，难道我们没有正当理由感到自己被涉及……当这可能是关于杜潘将自己从信的象征回路中抽身……我们这些人成为所有被偷的信件的使者，这些信件至少在一段时间内将在转移中于我们这里处于搁置状态？难道他们的转移所包含的责任，不是我们通过将其等同于最消解一切意义的能指——即金钱——来中和？但这还不是全部。杜潘从其绝技中所如此轻松地获取的利润，如果其目的是为了抽身其外，那就使他突然对大臣所采取的攻击——让我们说是一记暗招——显得更加悖论，甚至令人不快；尽管他刚刚对大臣所玩的把戏似乎已足以使其傲慢的声望泄气。

<!-- id: s2-17-0248 -->

我们已经说过那些残暴的诗句，他声称无法克制不题献，在他伪造的信中，在大臣被王后不可避免的挑衅所激怒、以为可以将其击败并冲入深渊的时刻：*facilis descensus Averni*【下至冥府易】[^48]，他训诫道，补充说大臣不可能不认出他的笔迹，这——为了在不冒风险的情况下留下无情的污名——看起来——针对一个并非无价值的人物——是一种不光彩的胜利【参高乃依：《熙德》第二幕第二场："*在没有危险的情况下获胜，是以无荣耀的胜利*"】，而他仍援引在维也纳（是在那次大会上吗？）所遭遇的不快所引发的怨恨，这只是在其上添加了额外的阴险。

<!-- id: s2-17-0249 -->

然而让我们更仔细地审视这一激情的爆发，尤其是它发生于一个本应靠冷静的头脑取得成功的行为之后。它恰恰发生在信的辨认之决定性行动已完成的那一刻，可以说杜潘已经掌握信如同已经取得它，但仍未能够脱手。

<!-- id: s2-17-0250 -->

*因此他*确实是*主体间三位一体*的参与者，并且作为如此，*处于先前被* *王后和大* *臣所占的中位*。当他显示出对这一位置的超越时，他是否同时向我们揭示*作者*的意图？如果他成功地将*信*放回其正途，他仍需让它到达其地址。而这个地址是先前由国王所占的位置，因为正是在那里它应该回到律法的秩序中。我们已经看到，无论是国王，还是接替这个位置的警察，都无法读它，因为这个*位置预设了盲目*。*Rex et augur*【国王与占卜师】[^49]，这些词的神话式古风，似乎只为我们感受在这里召唤一个人是何其荒诞而鸣响。而历史中的人物在这方面已经许久没有给我们多少鼓励。让人独自承担最高能指的重量，这并非人的自然倾向。而他来占据并披上这一位置的位置，也同样可能成为最大愚蠢的象征[^50]。

<!-- id: s2-17-0251 -->

我们可以说，国王在此被圣所固有的双义性所赋予，赋予了他的愚蠢，而这恰好属于主体。正是这一点将赋予那些将接替其位置的人物以意义。不是说警察可以被视为天生的文盲，我们知道在国家诞生之际，将长矛钉在*营地*【campus】上的作用[^51]。但在此行使其职能的警察完全带有自由主义的形态，也就是说，那些由不太关心压抑其不检点癖好的主人所强加于它的形态。因此人们偶尔并不避讳地谈及为其保留的权限：

<!-- id: s2-17-0252 -->

"*Sutor ne ultra crepidam*【鞋匠莫过制鞋线】，管好你们的小偷。我们甚至愿意为此给你们，*科学手段*。这将帮助你们不去想那些最好留在阴影中的真相。*" [^52]

<!-- id: s2-17-0253 -->

人们知道，从如此明智的原则中得到的宽慰，在历史中只持续了一个早晨的长度，并且命运的进程已经到处带回了——对自由统治之正当渴求的延续——对那些以罪行扰乱它的人的关注，这种关注甚至会适时地制造证据。

<!-- id: s2-17-0254 -->

我们甚至可以看到，这种*只为多数人*行使的实践，总是受到欢迎，现在却由那些可能提出异议者公开承认其伪造而得到证实：这是*能指*对*主体*之优先性的最新表现。尽管如此，警察档案始终是某种保留的对象，令人不解的是这种保留远不止于史学家的圈子。

<!-- id: s2-17-0255 -->

正是这种正在消逝的信用，杜潘打算向警察总监交付信的做法，将缩减其范围。当能指已经卸下对王后的讯息时，它从大臣手中一出来就其文本被宣告无效，所剩的还有什么呢？

<!-- id: s2-17-0256 -->

对它而言，所剩下的无非是回答这同一问题：*当一个能指不再具有意义时，它还剩下什么*。

<!-- id: s2-17-0257 -->

而这正是那个杜潘现在在*标志盲目的位置*重新遇到的人所询问的同一个问题。如果大臣确如人们所说的是那个赌徒，且他的行为已足以暴露他，那么这正是将他引向那里的问题。因为*赌徒的激情*不是别的，正是*对能指所提出的这一问题*，它由*偶然的自动发生*【αύτόματον automaton】所代表。

<!-- id: s2-17-0258 -->

> "*你是什么，骰子的形象，我在与我的命运的相遇*（τύχη【tuké】）[^53]*中翻转你？*什么也不是，唯有这种死亡的在场，它使人类生活成为一种每天早上以你的符号为牧杖的名义所获得的缓刑。* 
>
> *山鲁佐德【Schéhérazade】在千零一夜中如此行事，而我从十八个月以来也如此行事，以体验这一符号的威势*，
>
> *代价是在奇偶游戏中一系列令人眩晕的舞弊出招*"。

<!-- id: s2-17-0259 -->

因此杜潘，从其所处之位，无法抵御那个如此发问的人所引发的一种显然带有女性气质的愤怒。那位诗人的发明与数学家的严谨相结合，又伴以花花公子的冷静与作弊者的优雅的高超形象，突然之间，对于那个让我们品味它的人来说，变成了真正的*monstrum horrendum*（可怕怪物）——这是他自己的话——"*一个没有原则的天才*"。

<!-- id: s2-17-0260 -->

这里标示出这种恐怖的起源，而体验到它的人根本不需要以最出人意料的方式宣称自己为"*女方的拥护者*"来向我们揭示：众所周知，女士们讨厌原则被质疑，因为她们的魅力很大程度上归功于能指的神秘。

<!-- id: s2-17-0261 -->

因此杜潘终于将向我们转过*这个能指的蛇发女怪般的面孔*——除了王后，没有人能读到的只是其反面。引文的套语适合作为这张脸在其怪相中所带的神谕，并且它借自悲剧：

<!-- id: s2-17-0262 -->

> *……如此致命的命运，*
>
> *如果配不上阿特柔斯【Atrée】，至少配得上梯厄斯忒斯【Thyeste】。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0263 -->

这就是能指在所有意义之外的回答：

<!-- id: s2-17-0264 -->

> "*你自以为在行动，而我却在按我所系结的慘望之线索的摆布中驱动你。*
>
> *于是这些慘望就在力量中增长，并增殖为那些将你带回你那被撕裂的童年的碎片化的对象中。*
>
> *好，那就让这成为你的盛宴，直至那石客【l'invité de pierre】——既然你召唤了我，我将对你而言——的归来。*"

<!-- id: s2-17-0265 -->

为了回到一个更为温和的语调，让我们按照那个恶作剧来说——去年我们与你们中某些在苏黎世大会上同行的人，曾以此向那地方的暗语致敬——能指对发问者的回答是：*"吃掉你的此在【Dasein】*"。

<!-- id: s2-17-0266 -->

那么，这就是在那个决定性的约会中等待大臣的吗？杜潘向我们保证，但我们也已学会不让自己太过轻信他的种种转移。无疑这就是那个大胆者，沦于一种愚蠢的盲目状态，人相对于那些规定其命运的墙上的字母【מנא ,מנא, תקל, ופרסין：*弥尼，弥尼，提客勒，毗勒斯*】所处的状态。

<!-- id: s2-17-0267 -->

<img src="assets/image27.jpeg" style="width:2.11667in;height:1.68546in" alt="C:\Users\Alain\Desktop\Lacan séminaires\Ressources\Lettre volée\Illustrations\Rembrandt-Belsazar.jpg" />

<!-- id: s2-17-0268 -->

但是为了让他赴约，对于一个像他这样的人，能期待来自王后的单方面挑衅产生什么效果呢？爱或者恨。

<!-- id: s2-17-0269 -->

- 两者之一是盲目的，这将让他缴械投降。

<!-- id: s2-17-0270 -->

- 另一个是清醒的，但会唤起他的怀疑。

<!-- id: s2-17-0271 -->

但如果他确实如人们所说的那个赌徒，他会在打败它们之前，最后一次审问他的牌，并从中读出自己的牌局，他将从桌边及时起身以免耻辱。这就是一切吗？我们必须相信我们已经破译了杜潘越过他用来迷惑我们的那些想象性伎俩之外的真正策略吗？

<!-- id: s2-17-0272 -->

无疑是的，因为如果——正如杜潘首先宣称——"*任何需要反思的要点*"*在黑暗中最为有利地呈现给审视*，我们现在可以在大白天轻易读出其解答。

<!-- id: s2-17-0273 -->

它已经包含在我们故事标题中，并且容易从中提取，按照我们早就提交给你们裁断的公式，根据主体间交流——我们在其中告诉你们，发射者从接收者那里以颠倒的形式收到他自己的讯息。

<!-- id: s2-17-0274 -->

这就是*"被偷的信"*，甚至*"在途中的信"*所意味的：即*一封信总能达到其目的地*。

<!-- id: s2-17-0166 -->

- 人们悲叹*不再有*爱，

<!-- id: s2-17-0167 -->

- 人们累积*起*恨，

<!-- id: s2-17-0168 -->

- 而人们消耗*着*奉献，

<!-- id: s2-17-0169 -->

- 而如此多的自负安然接受总会有*贱卖的大腿*这一事实，

<!-- id: s2-17-0171 -->

但至于"信"，人们可以把它当作排版元素、信件或文人所成的东西来理解，我们可以说：

<!-- id: s2-17-0172 -->

- 人们所说的应该*照字面*理解，

<!-- id: s2-17-0173 -->

- 邮政收发室*有您一封信*等着您，

<!-- id: s2-17-0175 -->

让我们更仔细地看看警察发生了什么。关于他们如何搜查其调查所投入的空间的手段，他们毫不留情地向我们展示：

<!-- id: s2-17-0176 -->

- 把这个空间划分成不遗漏任何厚度的体积，

<!-- id: s2-17-0177 -->

- 用针探测松软处，

<!-- id: s2-17-0179 -->

随着他们的网络收紧，以至于他们不满足于抖动书页、还去数页数，我们难道没有看到空间像书页一样剥离吗？但研究者们有一种如此一成不变的*实在*观念，以至于他们没有注意到，他们的研究正走向将其实在转化为其*对象*。他们或许可以通过这一特征将*此对象*与其他所有对象区分开来。这也许对他们要求过高，无疑不是因为他们缺乏眼光，而正是因为我们缺乏眼光。

<!-- id: s2-17-0181 -->

问题在于，我们只能照字面这样说，*缺少在其位置上*的*只是能改变它的东西*，即*象征界的*。因为*对于实在界*，无论我们可能带来什么样的颠覆，*它总在那里*【在其位置上】而无论如何，*它粘在其足迹上被带走，不知道有什么能够流放它。*实际上，再说回到我们的警察，那些拿走了*信*、却是在*信被藏的位置上拿走它*的人，他们怎么可能抓住*信*呢？在他们手指间转动的东西中，他们所握有的，不是别的，就是不回应他们所拥有的那个特征描述的东西吗？

<!-- id: s2-17-0186 -->

"*这份文件，在一位第三者面前被揭示*

<!-- id: s2-17-0187 -->

——他将对其名字保持沉默，那个名字像父亲于布【UBU】牙齿之间的猪尾巴一样跃入眼帘。

<!-- id: s2-17-0189 -->

因此，使它流通的，不仅仅是意义，而是讯息的文本，而越是看似无害的，流通就越危险，因为其风险会因某位保管人可能在不知情的情况下犯下的不慎行为而增加。因此，没有什么能拯救警察的立场，而改善"*其文化*"也无济于事。*文字常驻*【Scripta manent】，它从一种装帧精美的人文主义那里学到的格言是徒劳的，而格言则以*言语飞逝*【verba volant】结束。【*言语飞逝，文字常驻：les paroles s'envolent, les écrits restent*】

<!-- id: s2-17-0198 -->

- 词缀"*pur-*"，我们可在*purpose：意图、purchase：采购、purport：含义*中再次发现，

<!-- id: s2-17-0199 -->

- 以及古法语词*"loing"*，*loigner：经过*。

<!-- id: s2-17-0201 -->

正是这样，我们在我们所投身的事物本身之中看到对我们的*迂回*的确认，因为这确实就是*被绕行的信*，其路线被*延长*了——字面上即是英语的延长——或者用邮政词汇来说，*在途中的信*【lettre en souffrance】。

<!-- id: s2-17-0213 -->

- 招致那个支持它的人受到*惩罚*或*犯罪*，这两者都打破了他对*律法*的附庸关系。

<!-- id: s2-17-0216 -->

- 可以向王后呈递恭敬的劝诫，他必须通过适当的保证确保其回返效果，

<!-- id: s2-17-0217 -->

- 或者对信的作者采取某种行动，他在游戏中保持置身事外这一事实表明，这里所涉及的几乎不是罪行和过错，而是*信所构成的矛盾和丑闻的标记*，按福音书中所说的，它必须发生，而不顾成为其载体的人的灾祸，

<!-- id: s2-17-0218 -->

- 甚至将已经成为*案卷中一份文件*的信交给"第三者"，他有权决定是否就此为王后召开火刑法庭，还是让大臣失势。

<!-- id: s2-17-0221 -->

- 延长其迂回以使它通过一次额外的转运到达应有的人那里，也就是说，通过另一种背叛，由于信的严重性，要防止其回返是困难的，

<!-- id: s2-17-0222 -->

- 或者销毁信，这是唯一可靠的方式，杜潘【DUPIN】一开始就这样说。

<!-- id: s2-17-0224 -->

大臣从局势中获取的威势，因此并不来自信本身，而是——无论他是否知道——来自信为他构成的人物。警长的言论也把他呈现为一个敢于做一切的人：*"*[*who da*<span id="R_who_dares_all_things" class="anchor"></span>*res all things*](#who_dares_all_things)，人们意味深长地评论道：*...those unbecoming as well as those becoming a man"*。这是说：*"不配作为一个人的，正如配作为一个人的"*，而波德莱尔在翻译时漏掉了其精妙之处：*"不配一个人的，正如配得上他的"*。因为在原文形式中，这个评语更适用于"*让一个女人感兴趣的东西*"。这让人看到这个人物的*想象界的范畴*，也就是说大臣所卷入的*自恋关系*，这一次当然是他不知情的。它也由英文文本中第二页叙述者的一句妙趣横生的话所指出：

<!-- id: s2-17-0225 -->

"*他告诉我们，大臣所获取的威势，依赖于劫持者对受害者认识劫持者这件事的认识。*"*原文为：*"*[*the ro*<span id="R_the_robber" class="anchor"></span>*bber's knowledge of the loser's knowledge of the robber.*](#the_robber)*"*

<!-- id: s2-17-0226 -->

作者让杜潘【DUPIN】在紧接着信被劫的场景叙事之后，以原文方式重复这些词，从而强调其重要性。这里我们仍可以说波德莱尔在其语言中游移，他让一个发问，另一个以这些词确认：

<!-- id: s2-17-0227 -->

- *"窃贼知道……"*然后

<!-- id: s2-17-0228 -->

- *"窃贼知道*——什么？——*被偷的人认识他的窃贼。"*

<!-- id: s2-17-0229 -->

因为对窃贼来说重要的不仅仅是该人知道*谁偷了他*，而是就窃贼而言他在与谁打交道，是他相信他能"*做一切*"，这必须理解为：她赋予他一个位置，这个位置没有任何人真正能够承担，因为它是*想象界的*，即绝对主人的位置。

<!-- id: s2-17-0230 -->

实际上这是一个绝对软弱的位置，但并非对于被让人去相信它的人而言。证明这一点不仅仅是王后有勇气求助于警察。因为她所做的只是顺从于她从起始三位一体的一行中位移一格，将自己托付给占据这个位置所必需的盲目本身：

<!-- id: s2-17-0231 -->

"[*No more saga*<span id="R_No_more_sagacious" class="anchor"></span>*cious agent could, I suppose*](#No_more_sagacious)——杜潘讽刺地说——*be desired or even imagined.*"

<!-- id: s2-17-0232 -->

不，她之所以跨出这一步，与其说是如人们所说的被*推向绝望*【*driven to despair*】，不如说是承担了一种不耐烦，这种不耐烦更应该归因于一种镜像的幻象。因为大臣在克制自己不去采取当时他所命定的不行动方面，是有相当多的事要做的。

<!-- id: s2-17-0233 -->

*大臣*实际上*并不完全疯*。这是警长的一句评论，而他总是*说话如金*：诚然，*他话语之金*只为杜潘而流，并且其流动只在*五万法郎*——按当时金属的标准，他为此付出的代价——的额度上才停止，*尽管这不会不给他留下有盈余的余额*。*因此大臣在这种疯狂的停滞中并不完全疯*，*这就是为什么他必须以神经症的方式行动*：

<!-- id: s2-17-0234 -->

- 如同那个退居小岛以遗忘的人——遗忘什么？——他遗忘了……

<!-- id: s2-17-0235 -->

- 如同大臣，因不使用信，反而将它遗忘。

<!-- id: s2-17-0236 -->

他行为的坚持性所表达的正是这一点。但信，和神经症的无意识一样，并不遗忘他。它如此不遗忘他，*以至于它将他*越来越转化为那个以他的惊讶呈上它的人的影像，并且他将以她为榜样，屈从于一种类似的惊讶。

<!-- id: s2-17-0237 -->

这种转化的特征被记录下来，并且以一种在它们表面的无理由中足够特征性的形式，足以有效地与*被压抑者的回归*相比拟。因此我们首先得知*他继而也翻过信*，当然不是以王后匆忙的手势，而是以一种更为精心操作的方式，如同人们翻转一件衣服那样。事实上，根据当时人们*折叠*一封信并*封蜡*的方式，他必须如此操作，以露出空白的部分来写新地址[^46]。这个地址成为他自己的地址。无论是他亲手所写还是由他人代笔，它都将以非常纤细的女性字迹出现，火漆印从激情的红色变到他镜子的黑色，他盖上自己的印章。

<!-- id: s2-17-0238 -->

这种*盖有收信人印章的信*的独特性，在其虚构中越是值得注意，因为它在文本中被强烈地表达出来，而在杜潘关于信的辨识的讨论中却甚至没有被他提及。无论这一遗漏是故意还是无意的，在一种我们看到其精细严密的创作的安排中，它都会令人惊讶。但无论如何，意味深长的是，大臣大致上是写给自己的信，是一封女性的信：仿佛他必须经历一个能指的天然相宜性的阶段。同样地……

<!-- id: s2-17-0239 -->

- *懒散的气场*甚至影响到柔弱的表象，

<!-- id: s2-17-0240 -->

- *无聊的铺陈*在他的言语中近于厌恶，

<!-- id: s2-17-0241 -->

- *氛围*——《*家具哲学*》【La philosophie de l'ameublement】[^47]的作者知道如何通过几乎不可触摸的笔触唤起的，诸如桌上那件乐器的笔触——*……*所有一切都似乎是协调一致的，以至于这个被其所有言语刻画了男性气概的人物，在他出现时散发出最独特的*女性气息*。如果这是一种伎俩，杜潘确实不忘记强调它，他告诉我们，在这种*假象*背后，是猛兽的警觉，准备一跃而起。

<!-- id: s2-17-0242 -->

但这就是无意识本身的效果，在我们教导其精确意义上无意识即是——人被能指所寓居——我们如何能找到比爱伦·坡【POE】自己所锻造来让我们理解杜潘的绝技的更美的形象呢？因为他为此借助了那些*地名*，一张地理地图为了让其不沉默，将其叠加在其图案之上，人们可以将其作为猜谜游戏的对象，看谁能找到一位伙伴所选择的那个；继而注意到最有可能使初学者迷失的，是地图上以宽间距的大写字母印出的、常常甚至目光都不停留的、标示出整个国家名称的那个。

<!-- id: s2-17-0243 -->

如此，*被偷的信*，如同一具庞大的女性身体，在大臣的办公室空间中铺开，当杜潘进入时。但他已预期在此找到它，他只需用*绿色眼镜*所遮掩的眼睛，剥去这具巨大的身体。正因如此，不需要，也因为没有机会，偷听弗洛伊德【FREUD】教授的门口，他会直奔那躺着、且正是这身体被做来藏匿的地方，在某个目光溜进去的美好中点，乃至在被诱惑者称为"*圣天使堡*"的地点，在他们天真地自以为由此把握全城的错觉之中。

<!-- id: s2-17-0244 -->

看！*在壁炉的两柱之间*，是这一伸手可及的对象，劫持者只需伸手去取……问题在于*他是抓住*——正如波德莱尔所译——*在壁炉台上*，还是【在壁炉台*下*】*sous le manteau*，如同原文所载，可以毫无损失地留给厨房的推断。

<!-- id: s2-17-0245 -->

如果*象征的效力*就此停止，是否因为*象征的债务*也已在其中消亡？如果我们能相信这一点，我们将被两个事件所警告其相反——这两个事件我们越不能视为附属，因为它们初看似乎与作品格格不入。首先是杜潘的酬金故事，它远非结尾的玩笑，它在开篇即由他向警长就*许诺给他的酬金数额*所提出的相当放肆的问题所预告，而警长虽然对自己的具体数字有所保留，却不打算向他隐瞒其巨大数额，并在后面回到其增长。

<!-- id: s2-17-0246 -->

杜潘此前被呈现为困居于以太之中的穷人，这一事实反而促使我们反思他在信的交付上所达成的交易，而他的*支票簿*的出现利落地保证了执行。我们并不认为无关紧要的是，他所引入的那个毫不隐晦的*暗示*，是"*归诸一位同样著名而古怪的人物*"——波德莱尔告诉我们——一位名叫阿伯内西【ABERNETHY】的英国医生的故事，其中涉及一个富有的守财奴，他想要从他那里免费获得一次诊治，结果得到的不是让他服药，而是让他接受建议。

<!-- id: s2-17-0247 -->

事实上，难道我们没有正当理由感到自己被涉及……当这可能是关于杜潘将自己从信的象征回路中抽身……我们这些人成为所有被偷的信件的使者，这些信件至少在一段时间内将在转移中于我们这里处于搁置状态？难道他们的转移所包含的责任，不是我们通过将其等同于最消解一切意义的能指——即金钱——来中和？但这还不是全部。杜潘从其绝技中所如此轻松地获取的利润，如果其目的是为了抽身其外，那就使他突然对大臣所采取的攻击——让我们说是一记暗招——显得更加悖论，甚至令人不快；尽管他刚刚对大臣所玩的把戏似乎已足以使其傲慢的声望泄气。

<!-- id: s2-17-0248 -->

我们已经说过那些残暴的诗句，他声称无法克制不题献，在他伪造的信中，在大臣被王后不可避免的挑衅所激怒、以为可以将其击败并冲入深渊的时刻：*facilis descensus Averni*【下至冥府易】[^48]，他训诫道，补充说大臣不可能不认出他的笔迹，这——为了在不冒风险的情况下留下无情的污名——看起来——针对一个并非无价值的人物——是一种不光彩的胜利【参高乃依：《熙德》第二幕第二场："*在没有危险的情况下获胜，是以无荣耀的胜利*"】，而他仍援引在维也纳（是在那次大会上吗？）所遭遇的不快所引发的怨恨，这只是在其上添加了额外的阴险。

<!-- id: s2-17-0249 -->

然而让我们更仔细地审视这一激情的爆发，尤其是它发生于一个本应靠冷静的头脑取得成功的行为之后。它恰恰发生在信的辨认之决定性行动已完成的那一刻，可以说杜潘已经掌握信如同已经取得它，但仍未能够脱手。

<!-- id: s2-17-0250 -->

*因此他*确实是*主体间三位一体*的参与者，并且作为如此，*处于先前被* *王后和大* *臣所占的中位*。当他显示出对这一位置的超越时，他是否同时向我们揭示*作者*的意图？如果他成功地将*信*放回其正途，他仍需让它到达其地址。而这个地址是先前由国王所占的位置，因为正是在那里它应该回到律法的秩序中。我们已经看到，无论是国王，还是接替这个位置的警察，都无法读它，因为这个*位置预设了盲目*。*Rex et augur*【国王与占卜师】[^49]，这些词的神话式古风，似乎只为我们感受在这里召唤一个人是何其荒诞而鸣响。而历史中的人物在这方面已经许久没有给我们多少鼓励。让人独自承担最高能指的重量，这并非人的自然倾向。而他来占据并披上这一位置的位置，也同样可能成为最大愚蠢的象征[^50]。

<!-- id: s2-17-0251 -->

我们可以说，国王在此被圣所固有的双义性所赋予，赋予了他的愚蠢，而这恰好属于主体。正是这一点将赋予那些将接替其位置的人物以意义。不是说警察可以被视为天生的文盲，我们知道在国家诞生之际，将长矛钉在*营地*【campus】上的作用[^51]。但在此行使其职能的警察完全带有自由主义的形态，也就是说，那些由不太关心压抑其不检点癖好的主人所强加于它的形态。因此人们偶尔并不避讳地谈及为其保留的权限：

<!-- id: s2-17-0252 -->

"*Sutor ne ultra crepidam*【鞋匠莫过制鞋线】，管好你们的小偷。我们甚至愿意为此给你们，*科学手段*。这将帮助你们不去想那些最好留在阴影中的真相。*" [^52]

<!-- id: s2-17-0253 -->

人们知道，从如此明智的原则中得到的宽慰，在历史中只持续了一个早晨的长度，并且命运的进程已经到处带回了——对自由统治之正当渴求的延续——对那些以罪行扰乱它的人的关注，这种关注甚至会适时地制造证据。

<!-- id: s2-17-0254 -->

我们甚至可以看到，这种*只为多数人*行使的实践，总是受到欢迎，现在却由那些可能提出异议者公开承认其伪造而得到证实：这是*能指*对*主体*之优先性的最新表现。尽管如此，警察档案始终是某种保留的对象，令人不解的是这种保留远不止于史学家的圈子。

<!-- id: s2-17-0255 -->

正是这种正在消逝的信用，杜潘打算向警察总监交付信的做法，将缩减其范围。当能指已经卸下对王后的讯息时，它从大臣手中一出来就其文本被宣告无效，所剩的还有什么呢？

<!-- id: s2-17-0256 -->

对它而言，所剩下的无非是回答这同一问题：*当一个能指不再具有意义时，它还剩下什么*。

<!-- id: s2-17-0257 -->

而这正是那个杜潘现在在*标志盲目的位置*重新遇到的人所询问的同一个问题。如果大臣确如人们所说的是那个赌徒，且他的行为已足以暴露他，那么这正是将他引向那里的问题。因为*赌徒的激情*不是别的，正是*对能指所提出的这一问题*，它由*偶然的自动发生*【αύτόματον automaton】所代表。

<!-- id: s2-17-0258 -->

> "*你是什么，骰子的形象，我在与我的命运的相遇*（τύχη【tuké】）[^53]*中翻转你？*什么也不是，唯有这种死亡的在场，它使人类生活成为一种每天早上以你的符号为牧杖的名义所获得的缓刑。* 
>
> *山鲁佐德【Schéhérazade】在千零一夜中如此行事，而我从十八个月以来也如此行事，以体验这一符号的威势*，
>
> *代价是在奇偶游戏中一系列令人眩晕的舞弊出招*"。

<!-- id: s2-17-0259 -->

因此杜潘，从其所处之位，无法抵御那个如此发问的人所引发的一种显然带有女性气质的愤怒。那位诗人的发明与数学家的严谨相结合，又伴以花花公子的冷静与作弊者的优雅的高超形象，突然之间，对于那个让我们品味它的人来说，变成了真正的*monstrum horrendum*（可怕怪物）——这是他自己的话——"*一个没有原则的天才*"。

<!-- id: s2-17-0260 -->

这里标示出这种恐怖的起源，而体验到它的人根本不需要以最出人意料的方式宣称自己为"*女方的拥护者*"来向我们揭示：众所周知，女士们讨厌原则被质疑，因为她们的魅力很大程度上归功于能指的神秘。

<!-- id: s2-17-0261 -->

因此杜潘终于将向我们转过*这个能指的蛇发女怪般的面孔*——除了王后，没有人能读到的只是其反面。引文的套语适合作为这张脸在其怪相中所带的神谕，并且它借自悲剧：

<!-- id: s2-17-0262 -->

> *……如此致命的命运，*
>
> *如果配不上阿特柔斯【Atrée】，至少配得上梯厄斯忒斯【Thyeste】。*

<!-- id: s2-17-0263 -->

这就是能指在所有意义之外的回答：

<!-- id: s2-17-0264 -->

> "*你自以为在行动，而我却在按我所系结的慘望之线索的摆布中驱动你。*
>
> *于是这些慘望就在力量中增长，并增殖为那些将你带回你那被撕裂的童年的碎片化的对象中。*
>
> *好，那就让这成为你的盛宴，直至那石客【l'invité de pierre】——既然你召唤了我，我将对你而言——的归来。*"

<!-- id: s2-17-0265 -->

为了回到一个更为温和的语调，让我们按照那个恶作剧来说——去年我们与你们中某些在苏黎世大会上同行的人，曾以此向那地方的暗语致敬——能指对发问者的回答是：*"吃掉你的此在【Dasein】*"。

<!-- id: s2-17-0266 -->

那么，这就是在那个决定性的约会中等待大臣的吗？杜潘向我们保证，但我们也已学会不让自己太过轻信他的种种转移。无疑这就是那个大胆者，沦于一种愚蠢的盲目状态，人相对于那些规定其命运的墙上的字母【מנא ,מנא, תקל, ופרסין：*弥尼，弥尼，提客勒，毗勒斯*】所处的状态。

<!-- id: s2-17-0267 -->

<img src="assets/image27.jpeg" style="width:2.11667in;height:1.68546in" alt="C:\Users\Alain\Desktop\Lacan séminaires\Ressources\Lettre volée\Illustrations\Rembrandt-Belsazar.jpg" />

<!-- id: s2-17-0268 -->

但是为了让他赴约，对于一个像他这样的人，能期待来自王后的单方面挑衅产生什么效果呢？爱或者恨。

<!-- id: s2-17-0269 -->

- 两者之一是盲目的，这将让他缴械投降。

<!-- id: s2-17-0270 -->

- 另一个是清醒的，但会唤起他的怀疑。

<!-- id: s2-17-0271 -->

但如果他确实如人们所说的那个赌徒，他会在打败它们之前，最后一次审问他的牌，并从中读出自己的牌局，他将从桌边及时起身以免耻辱。这就是一切吗？我们必须相信我们已经破译了杜潘越过他用来迷惑我们的那些想象性伎俩之外的真正策略吗？

<!-- id: s2-17-0272 -->

无疑是的，因为如果——正如杜潘首先宣称——"*任何需要反思的要点*"*在黑暗中最为有利地呈现给审视*，我们现在可以在大白天轻易读出其解答。

<!-- id: s2-17-0273 -->

它已经包含在我们故事标题中，并且容易从中提取，按照我们早就提交给你们裁断的公式，根据主体间交流——我们在其中告诉你们，发射者从接收者那里以颠倒的形式收到他自己的讯息。

<!-- id: s2-17-0274 -->

这就是*"被偷的信"*，甚至*"在途中的信"*所意味的：即*一封信总能达到其目的地*。

<!-- id: s2-17-0275 -->

埃德加·爱伦·坡【Edgar Alan POE】：[*被偷的信*](#Table) \[[返回正文\](#R_Lettre)\] *Nil sapientiæ odiosius acumine nimio*【过度的机敏，对智慧最为有害】。

<!-- id: s2-17-0286 -->

这必然导致他藐视所有平庸的藏匿处。这样一个人不可能软弱到无法猜到，对他府邸中最复杂、最深的藏匿处，对于警长的眼睛、探针、钻头和显微镜来说，与一间前厅或一个衣柜一样毫不隐秘。最后我看到，如果他不是出于自然的喜好所诱导，他必然要追求简单。你们无疑记得，在我们的首次会面中，警长以怎样的笑声来迎接我所表达的想法，即如果这谜团如此困扰他，那也许正是由于其绝对的简单。——是的，我说，我完全记得他的狂笑。我真的以为他要歇斯底里发作了。——物质世界，杜潘继续说，与非物质的世界充满精确的类比，这给了修辞学教条一种真实的色彩，即一个隐喻或比较可以像美化描述一样加强论证。例如，惯性力的原则在物理和形而上学的两种本性中似乎都是相同的；一个大的物体比一个小的物体更难被推动，其动量与这种难度成比例；这与下列类似的命题一样是肯定的：那些拥有巨大容量的智力——它们在运动中比低一级的智力更猛烈、更持续、更曲折——恰恰是那些最不容易运动、并且在起步时最被犹豫所困扰的。另一个例子：你们可曾注意过，哪些是那些最吸引注意力的店铺招牌？

<!-- id: s2-17-0287 -->

——我从未想到过这一点，我说。——存在一个，杜潘接着说，是用地图来玩的占卜游戏。一个玩家请某个人猜一个给定的词，一个城市、河流、州或帝国的名字，总之是包含在地图的杂色而混乱的范围中的任意一个词。一个游戏的初学者通常试图通过让对手猜写在难以辨认的字符中的名字来为难他们；但游戏的行家选择那些以大写字母印出、贯穿地图一端到另一端的词。这些词，正如那些巨大字母的招牌和海报一样，由于其过度的明显性本身而逃过了观察者的注意；这里，物质的遗忘正类似于一个精神的道德上的疏忽——一个让那些过于明显、明显到陈腐和烦人的考量溜走的疏忽。但据这看来，这是一个略高于或低于警长智力的案例。他从未相信或认为大臣可能将其信恰好放在全世界的鼻子底下，仿佛是为了更好地阻止任何个人看到它。

<!-- id: s2-17-0288 -->

但我越是反思这个大胆者、D……的独特而光辉的精神，他必然始终将文件放在手边，以便在需要时立即使用，以及另一个事实，根据警长所提供的决定性证明，这一文件并未藏匿于一次普通而正规的搜查范围之内……我越是确信，大臣为了藏匿其信，求助于世界上最巧妙的、最宽泛的计策，*即甚至不去尝试藏匿它*。

<!-- id: s2-17-0289 -->

在这些想法的驱使下，我在我眼中戴上一副绿色眼镜，并在某个美好的早晨，仿佛偶然地，前往大臣的府邸。我发现D……在家，打着哈欠、闲荡、消磨时光，声称自己被一种至高的无聊所压倒。D……也许是当今最真正有活力的人，但只有在确信无人看到他时才是如此。为了不输于他，我抱怨我眼睛的弱点以及戴眼镜的必要性。但在这副眼镜之后，我仔细而细致地检查了整个公寓，假装全神贯注地与我的主人交谈。我特别注意到一张他坐在旁边的大书桌，桌面上杂乱地放着各种信件和其他文件，伴着一两件乐器和几本书。在一次从容不迫的长时间检查后，我没有看到任何特别激起我怀疑的东西。终于，我的眼睛在扫视房间时，落在了一个可怜的卡片夹上，它装饰着一些假金箔，由一条脏兮兮的蓝色丝带悬挂在壁炉台上方的一个小铜钮上。这个卡片夹有三四个隔间，里面装着五六张名片和*一封单独的信*。这封信被严重弄脏并起皱。

<!-- id: s2-17-0290 -->

它几乎从中间被撕成两半，仿佛最初打算完全撕毁它，就像对待一个无价值的物品那样；但想法显然被改变了。它带有一个宽大的黑色火漆印，D……的徽记非常醒目地印在上面，并被寄给大臣本人。信封上的字是用非常纤细的女性字迹所写。它被随意地扔进去，甚至似乎是相当不屑地扔进卡片夹的上层隔间之一。

<!-- id: s2-17-0291 -->

我几乎没有瞥一眼这封信，就断定这就是我所寻找的那封。显然，从外观上看，它与警长向我们读了如此详细描述的那封完全不同。这里的火漆是宽而黑的，带有D……的徽记；在那封信中，它是小而红的，带有S……公爵家族的纹章。这里的字迹是一种细小而女性化的字迹；在那封信中，收信地址——上面写着一位王室人物的名字——是一种大胆、果敢且特征鲜明的字迹；两封信只在一点上相似：尺寸。

<!-- id: s2-17-0292 -->

但是这些根本性的差异的过度特征——污秽、纸张的糟糕状态、被揉皱和撕破——这些与D……有条不紊的真实习惯相矛盾，暴露了通过提供一个无价值文件的所有外观来误导一个好奇者的意图——再加上该文件被不谨慎地置于所有访客眼前的位置，与我先前的结论完全一致——所有这些，我再说一次，足以坚决支持一个带着怀疑的成见而来的人的怀疑。我尽可能延长我的访问，同时与大臣就一个我知道对他来说永远具有新意的议题展开一场非常激烈的讨论，我始终不移地让我的注意力锁定在信上。在进行这次检查时，我思考着它的外观以及它在卡片夹中的排列方式，最终我偶然发现了一个发现，使我还可能有的轻微怀疑化为乌有。在分析纸张的边缘时，我注意到它们比*自然状态*更为磨损。它们呈现一种硬纸张的断裂外观——这种纸张曾被折叠并被纸刀压过，然后按相反方向被重新折叠，但在构成其最初形状的同样折痕中。这个发现对我已经足够。信被像手套一样翻转、重新折叠并重新封印——这对我来说很清楚。我向大臣道了日安，突然向他告辞，在他的书桌上忘了一个金鼻烟壶。

<!-- id: s2-17-0293 -->

第二天早晨，我来取我的鼻烟壶，我们非常激烈地继续了前一天的谈话。但是，当讨论开始时，一声非常响亮的爆裂声——像是手枪声——在府邸的窗户下响起，紧接着是一群惊恐的人群的尖叫和叫喊。D……冲向一扇窗户，打开它，向街上张望。同时，我径直走向卡片夹，拿起信，放入口袋，并用另一封——一种（就外观而言）我已在家中仔细准备的*摹本*——替换它——用一块面包心火漆印伪造了D……的徽记【原文如此】。

<!-- id: s2-17-0294 -->

街上的喧嚣是由一个持枪男子的疯狂任性所引起的。他向一群妇女和儿童中间开了火。但由于枪未装实弹，人们认为这个怪人是一个疯子或醉汉，并让他继续前行。当他离开后，D……从窗户退开，在我确认得到那封珍贵的信之后，我立刻也跟了过去。

<!-- id: s2-17-0295 -->

片刻之后，我向他告别。这个所谓的疯子是由我雇来的人。——但是你用一封伪造品来替换信，你的目的是什么？我向我的朋友问道。在你第一次访问时，就把它拿走，不是更简单吗，无需其他预防措施，就离开？——D……，杜潘回答说，什么事都做得出，此外，他是一个结实的人。而且，他在他的府邸中有听命于他的仆人。如果我做了你所说的那种荒谬的尝试，我不可能活着走出他的家。巴黎的好人就不会再听到关于我的消息。但是，除了这些考量，我还有一个特别的目的。你们知道我的政治同情心。在这件事中，我作为这位女士的拥护者行事。十八个月以来，大臣一直将她握在他的权力之下。现在是她把他握住，因为他不知道信已经不在他那里，他将想要进行他通常的勒索。因此他必然地、一次性地亲手造成自己的政治毁灭。他的倒下不会比其可笑性更不迅疾。人们颇为轻率地谈论*facilis descensus Averni*【下至冥府易】；但在攀登的事情上，人们可以说出Catalani论歌唱时所说的：上去比下去容易。在目前的情况下，我对那个将要下去的人毫无同情，甚至没有怜悯。D……，那是真正的*monstrum horrendum*【可怕怪物】——一个没有原则的天才。然而，我向你们承认，我并不会不高兴知道他的思想的确切性质，当被警长所称的*某位女士*所挑衅，他将不得不打开我留在他的卡片夹中的那封信时。

<!-- id: s2-17-0296 -->

——怎么！你在里面放了什么特别的东西吗？——啊！我觉得让里面是空白的不太合适——那看起来像是一种侮辱。有一次，在维也纳，D……对我玩了一个卑鄙的伎俩，我以一种非常愉快的口气对他说我会记得这一点。而且，因为我料到他会对那个玩弄他的人产生某种好奇心，我认为如果不留给他某种线索，那真是可惜。他非常熟悉我的笔迹，我在空白页正中间抄录了这些字：

<!-- id: s2-17-0297 -->

> "……*一个如此致命的阴谋*，
>
> *如果配不上阿特柔斯【Atrée】，至少配得上梯厄斯忒斯【Thyeste】。*"

<!-- id: s2-17-0298 -->

你们可以在克雷比永【Crébillon】的《阿特柔斯》[^54]中找到这段话。
